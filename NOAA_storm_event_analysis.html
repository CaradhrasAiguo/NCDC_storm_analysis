NOAA Storm event analysis

KHYS / kshu

3 Apr 2018

Synopsis

The U.S. National Climatic Data Center (NCDC), a weather data archive under the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, maintains a database of severe weather events. It includes timing and estimates of location, fatalities, injuries, property and crop damage as well encodings for event type (EVTYPE) and prepared remarks. The overwhelming majority of the work supporting thisreport" was to clean the myriad EVTYPE variable, numbering nearly 900 types, into the several dozen types at the NCDC Storm Events database. Despite the clear imperfections resulting from this cleaning, the results inferred from the ggplot2-produced plots were not surprising: tornadoes, flooding, hurricanes/typhoons, and heat waves were among the most consequential events to public health and finances.

toMultiplier<-function(x) {
  return(case_when(x %in% c("","-","?") ~ 0,
                   x == "+" ~ 1,
                   x %in% as.character(seq(0,8)) ~ 10,
                   toupper(x) == "H" ~ 100,
                   toupper(x) == "K" ~ 1E3,
                   toupper(x) == "M" ~ 1E6,
                   toupper(x) == "B" ~ 1E9,
                   TRUE ~ as.numeric(NA)))
}

stormData<-stormData %>%

As an example, a hail event in Park County, Colorado on 1994-08-04 is listed with a PROPDMG of 4 and PROPDMGEXP of 0. Searching at the NCDC Storm Events database results in property damages of 0.04K. Thus, if PROPDMGEXP = 0, it is actually a multiplier of 10.

Reading the remarks, the NCDC characterizes the event in Florida as beginning with a severe squall line, which is by definition a line of thunderstorms. The narrative also notes multiple tornadoes and the rare (for Florida) winter storm surge, which are also valid event types. However, for the purpose of simplifying this analysis, the cleaned EVTYPE, stored in typeEvent, will be Thunderstorm Wind. The typeEvent is now renamed accordingly:

Before proceeding to other values of EVTYPE, 6.7% of the observations now have a cleaned typeEvent value. The code uses case_when(), similar to a switch and case clause series in Java.

  • Flash floods processed; the reader is invited to parse this Regex for themselves.
  • Non-flash floods (output of typeEvent="Flood") occur when there is no flash element or any coastal flooding or beach erosion.
  • The choice to classify EVTYPE values containing "COAST", "BEACH", and/or "EROSION", but not the word "SURGE" as being typeEvent="Coastal Flood" was somewhat arbitrary, as coastal flooding and beach erosion can clearly occur with Storm Surge.
  • For typeEvent="Thunderstorm Wind", the regular expression tStmRegex was chosen so that event descriptions beginning with SEVERE THUNDERSTORM, MARINE THUNDERSTORM, THUNDERSTORM, or TSTORM would take precedent, regardless of the presence of other possible events afterwards, such as EVTYPE="TSTM WIND/HAIL". This guideline will be observed henceforth unless otherwise stated.
  • Events containing WATERSP but not the TORN prefix are classified as typeEvent=Waterspout.
tStmRegex
## [1] "^(THU|TSTM|(SEV(.)+(THU|TSTM))|(GU(.)+(THU|TSTM)))"
stormData<-stormData %>%
  mutate(typeEvent=ifelse(!is.na(typeEvent),
                          typeEvent,
                          case_when(
                            grepl("(^FLASH)|(FLOOD(.){1}FLASH)",EVTYPE) |
                              grepl("( FLASH FLOOD)$",EVTYPE) ~
                              "Flash Flood",
                            grepl("FLOOD|FLD",EVTYPE) &
                              !grepl("(FLASH|COAST|BEACH|EROSION|THU)",
                                     EVTYPE) ~
                              "Flood",
                            grepl("(COAST|BEACH|EROSION)",EVTYPE) &
                              !grepl("SURGE",EVTYPE) ~
                              "Coastal Flood",
                            grepl("SURGE",EVTYPE) ~
                              "Storm Surge/Tide",
                            grepl(tStmRegex,EVTYPE) ~
                              "Thunderstorm Wind",
                            grepl(paste("^(MAR(.)+(",
                                        wordTstm,
                                        "))",
                                        sep=""),EVTYPE) ~
                              "Marine Thunderstorm Wind",
                            grepl("HUR|TYP",EVTYPE) ~
                              "Hurricane (Typhoon)",
                            grepl("TROP",EVTYPE) ~
                              "Tropical Storm or Depression",
                            grepl("(?=.*\\bWATERSP)(?!.*TORN)",
                                  EVTYPE,
                                  perl=TRUE) ~
                              "Waterspout",
                            TRUE ~ as.character(NA)
                          )
  )
  )

Clearly, landslides account for the great majority of the unprocessed million-dollar events. Since they can be caused by a multitude of factors beyond weather, including earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, or even deliberate activity, they should be excluded. Using ggplot2, and computing a summed aggregate over the entirety of 19502011, the worst event type by both total Fatalities and total Injuries was Tornado, with 5636 deaths and 91407 injuries. The worst event type by Total Damages (property and crop) was Flood, incurring a total of $161.09 billion.

The next graph will be a time series of total statistics by year. However, there are 36 classified event types, with many of them, such as Astronomical Low Tide being insignificant to health and/or damages in most years. Thus, to reduce clutter, an appropriate number of event types will be examined.

cumulatives<-apply(aggregates %>% select(-typeEvent),
